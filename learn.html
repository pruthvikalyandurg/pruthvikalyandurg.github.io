🌱 Plant Pathology

What is Plant Pathology?

Plants get sick — infected by bacteria, fungi, viruses, oomycetes, and more. Phytophthora infestans, the water mould behind the Irish Potato Famine, still destroys 10–15% of global potato harvests today. Plant pathogens collectively cost the world economy over $220 billion a year.

Key fact: Plant pathogens destroy an estimated 20–40% of global food crops annually.

Pathogens enter through wounds, stomata, or insect vectors, then hijack cellular machinery — suppressing immunity and breaking down tissue. Fungi like Botrytis cinerea are opportunistic; Phytophthora is aggressive enough to flatten entire fields within days.

My own work spans virus biology, RNA silencing, and spray-induced gene silencing (SIGS) — a technology that uses RNA molecules to switch off pathogen genes before infection takes hold. Today, as CTO of a biotech startup, I'm applying this science to post-harvest protection.

🧬 RNA Biology

What is RNA Interference (RNAi)?

RNAi is a natural gene-silencing mechanism — a cell's way of destroying specific RNA messages before they make proteins. Discovered in 1998 by Fire and Mello (Nobel Prize 2006), it's now one of the most powerful tools in both basic research and applied biotechnology.

The mechanism: Double-stranded RNA is cut into ~21-nucleotide fragments by Dicer. These guide a "scissors" complex (RISC) to matching RNA sequences and destroy them — silencing that gene.

My research used a related technology called Spray-Induced Gene Silencing (SIGS): design dsRNA matching a pathogen's essential genes, spray it on plants, and the pathogen silences itself. In our 2021 Phytopathology paper, we demonstrated this against Phytophthora infestans — the first time SIGS had been shown to work against this pathogen.

Conventional fungicides are failing as pathogens evolve resistance. SIGS-based protection is highly specific, leaves no chemical residue, and breaks down harmlessly — a sustainable alternative that works at the genetic level.

🔬 Microscopy

Why Microscopy Matters in Plant Science

A plant cell is 10–100 µm across — invisible without magnification, yet inside it viruses hijack ribosomes, fungi breach cell walls, and hormones redirect entire developmental programmes. Confocal fluorescence microscopy makes these events visible in real time.

Confocal microscopy uses laser light to excite fluorescent tags (GFP, RFP) attached to specific proteins. Tag different proteins different colours, and you can watch them interact inside a living cell. In my work on Potato mop-top virus, this revealed how the viral movement protein TGB1 recruits the host protein HIPP26 at the plasma membrane and redirects it to the nucleus — enabling long-distance virus spread.

Cool fact: GFP was originally isolated from a jellyfish (Aequorea victoria) and is now one of the most-used tools in all of biology.

There's also an aesthetic dimension. A confocal image — cytoplasmic strands in green, a glowing nucleus, fluorescent clusters at the membrane — is genuinely beautiful. Science and art both start with seeing clearly.

🌾 Global Impact

Plant Diseases and Global Food Security

Every year, plant diseases destroy enough food to feed hundreds of millions of people — and as climate change expands pathogen ranges, the problem is accelerating. Post-harvest losses alone account for 30–40% of food in developing countries.

Today's scale: For cut flowers, post-harvest disease losses can reach 40–50% of supply chain value.

Phytophthora infestans still destroys $6–7 billion in potato crops annually. Botrytis cinerea attacks strawberries, grapes, tomatoes, and cut flowers. Wheat stem rust, citrus greening, banana Fusarium wilt — the list of economically devastating pathogens is long and growing.

No single solution is sufficient. My own focus is on biological and RNA-based approaches — technologies that work with nature, leave no residue, and reduce the risk of resistance evolution.

🌿 Biocontrol

Biological Control of Plant Diseases

For every plant pathogen, there's often a natural enemy — a predatory fungus, a beneficial bacterium — that keeps it in check. Biocontrol deploys these organisms as alternatives to synthetic chemicals.

At SLU I studied Clonostachys rosea, a biocontrol fungus that parasitises plant pathogens. We found that cross-kingdom RNA signalling plays a key role in how it communicates with wheat roots and suppresses disease. I also characterised Pseudomonas fluorescens SLU99 — a beneficial bacterium that promotes plant growth and produces antimicrobial compounds by colonising roots. Understanding these interactions at the molecular level is what makes biocontrol reliable enough to deploy at scale.

🌱 Plant Biology

How Plants Develop

Plants are exquisitely responsive systems — constantly reading light, gravity, temperature, and chemical signals, and adjusting their architecture accordingly. My MSc research at Uppsala showed how abscisic acid (ABA) cross-talks with vascular development hormones in Arabidopsis roots.

ABA is best known as the stress hormone that closes stomata during drought — but our work revealed a subtler role: coordinating the hormonal networks that decide which cells become xylem and which become phloem. Root architecture determines water and nutrient uptake efficiency; plants with better roots yield more, tolerate drought better, and resist some pathogens more effectively.
