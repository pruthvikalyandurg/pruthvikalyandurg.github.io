🌱 Plant Pathology

What is Plant Pathology?

Plants, like animals, get sick. They are infected by bacteria, fungi, viruses, oomycetes, nematodes, and parasitic plants. The science of understanding, diagnosing, and managing these diseases is called plant pathology.

Plant diseases are not a modern problem. They have shaped human history. The Phytophthora infestans oomycete — often called a "water mould" — caused the Irish Potato Famine of 1845–1852, killing approximately one million people and forcing another million to emigrate. Today, this same organism still destroys around 10–15% of global potato harvests each year.

Key fact: Plant pathogens destroy an estimated 20–40% of global food crops annually, costing the world economy over $220 billion per year.

What causes plant diseases?

Pathogens enter plants through wounds, stomata (tiny pores on leaves), or by being injected by insect vectors like aphids. Once inside, they hijack the plant's cellular machinery — redirecting nutrients, suppressing immunity, and producing enzymes that break down plant tissue.

Fungi like Botrytis cinerea (grey mould) are opportunistic, attacking weakened or damaged tissues. Others, like Phytophthora, are highly specialised and aggressive — capable of destroying entire fields within days under the right conditions.

What do plant pathologists actually do?

My own career is a good example. As a plant pathologist, I have studied how viruses like Potato mop-top virus move between cells and suppress the plant's immune system. I have also pioneered the use of spray-induced gene silencing (SIGS) — a technology that uses RNA molecules to switch off pathogen genes and stop infection before it starts.

Today, as CTO of a biotech startup, I am applying this science to real-world post-harvest problems — protecting cut flowers and fresh produce from fungal disease without harmful chemicals.

🧬 RNA Biology

What is RNA Interference (RNAi)?

Every living cell uses a language written in molecules — DNA stores the instructions, RNA carries them, and proteins execute them. RNA interference (RNAi) is a natural cellular mechanism that intercepts and silences RNA messages before they can be executed — effectively switching off specific genes.

RNAi was discovered in 1998 by Andrew Fire and Craig Mello, who won the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for it in 2006. The discovery was revolutionary: it revealed that small RNA molecules — not proteins — could be the key regulators of gene expression in all living things.

The mechanism: Double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) is detected inside the cell and cut into short fragments (~21 nucleotides) by an enzyme called Dicer. These small RNAs guide a molecular "scissors" complex (RISC) to matching RNA sequences and destroy them — silencing that gene.

RNAi in crop protection

My research at SLU Alnarp focused on a technology called Spray-Induced Gene Silencing (SIGS). The idea: if we design dsRNA molecules that match essential genes in a pathogen, and spray them onto plant surfaces, the pathogen will absorb those RNA molecules and accidentally silence its own critical genes — stopping infection.

In our 2021 Phytopathology paper, we showed for the first time that SIGS can reduce Phytophthora infestans infection by targeting genes essential for its pathogenicity. This is a landmark result because it suggests we could protect crops from disease with RNA — a biological molecule that leaves no chemical residue and breaks down harmlessly in the environment.

Why this matters for the future of agriculture

Conventional fungicides are increasingly failing as pathogens evolve resistance. Regulatory pressure is reducing the number of approved chemicals. SIGS-based crop protection could offer a highly specific, sustainable, and environmentally safe alternative — one that works at the genetic level, targeting only the pathogen you want to stop.

🔬 Microscopy

Why Microscopy Matters in Plant Science

A plant cell is roughly 10–100 micrometres across — far too small to see without magnification. But inside that tiny space, extraordinary events unfold: viruses hijack ribosomes, fungi push through cell walls, and hormonal signals redirect the entire developmental programme of a shoot.

Microscopy is the technology that makes the invisible visible. Without it, plant science would remain largely theoretical. With it, we can watch pathogens invade in real time, track where proteins move inside living cells, and see how a single gene mutation changes the architecture of an entire root system.

Types of microscopy I use in my research

Confocal fluorescence microscopy is my main tool. It uses laser light to excite fluorescent molecules (like GFP, the Green Fluorescent Protein) attached to specific proteins. By tagging different proteins with different colours, I can see exactly where they are inside a cell and whether they interact with each other.

In my research on Potato mop-top virus, confocal microscopy was essential for showing how the viral movement protein TGB1 interacts with the plant protein HIPP26 at the plasma membrane and microtubules — redirecting it to the nucleus to facilitate long-distance virus spread.

Cool fact: The GFP gene used to make proteins glow green was originally isolated from a jellyfish (Aequorea victoria). It is now one of the most widely used tools in all of biology.

Microscopy as visual storytelling

There is also an aesthetic dimension to microscopy. A confocal image of a plant cell — showing cytoplasmic strands highlighted in green, a glowing nucleus, and fluorescent protein clusters at the cell membrane — is genuinely beautiful. Science and art are not opposites; they are both about seeing clearly and sharing what you see.

🌾 Global Impact

Plant Diseases and Global Food Security

Every year, plant diseases destroy enough food to feed hundreds of millions of people. This is not a hypothetical future problem — it is happening now. And as climate change shifts temperatures and rainfall patterns, many pathogens are expanding into regions where they have never been seen before.

The Irish Potato Famine is the most famous example of what happens when a crop meets a pathogen it cannot resist. In 1845, Phytophthora infestans arrived in Ireland — a population dependent on the potato as its primary food source. Within two years, the harvest had collapsed. The consequences — famine, mass death, emigration — reshaped the demographics of an entire nation.

Today's scale: Post-harvest losses — including disease in storage and transit — account for 30–40% of food loss in developing countries. For cut flowers, the figure can reach 40–50% of supply chain value.

The pathogens that keep scientists awake at night

Phytophthora infestans (potato late blight) still destroys an estimated $6–7 billion in potato crops annually. Botrytis cinerea (grey mould) attacks strawberries, grapes, tomatoes, and cut flowers, costing the global economy billions each year. Wheat stem rust, citrus greening, banana Fusarium wilt — the list goes on.

What can science do?

Resistance breeding, chemical control, biological control, and RNA-based technologies all have roles to play. No single approach is sufficient. My own work focuses on biological and RNA-based solutions — technologies that work with nature rather than against it, leaving no chemical residue and reducing the risk of resistance evolution.

🌿 Biocontrol

Biological Control of Plant Diseases

For every plant pathogen, there is often a natural enemy — a predatory fungus, a beneficial bacterium, or a competing organism that keeps the pathogen in check. Biocontrol is the science of finding and deploying these natural enemies as alternatives to synthetic chemicals.

My research at SLU included studying Clonostachys rosea, a biocontrol fungus that parasitises plant pathogens. We showed that RNA interference — and particularly cross-kingdom sRNA signalling — plays a crucial role in how C. rosea communicates with wheat roots and suppresses pathogens.

We also characterised beneficial bacteria like Pseudomonas fluorescens SLU99, which promotes plant growth and protects against disease by colonising roots and producing antimicrobial compounds. Understanding how these organisms work at the molecular level is key to deploying them reliably and effectively in agriculture.

🌱 Plant Biology

How Plants Develop

Plants are not passive organisms. They are exquisitely responsive systems — constantly monitoring light, temperature, gravity, touch, and chemical signals, and adjusting their growth accordingly.

My MSc thesis investigated how the plant hormone abscisic acid (ABA) influences root vascular development in Arabidopsis thaliana — the model plant of biology. ABA is best known as the stress hormone (it triggers stomata to close during drought), but our work showed it also plays a subtle, cross-talking role in coordinating the hormonal networks that specify which cells become xylem and which become phloem in the root.

Understanding development matters for agriculture: the architecture of a plant's root system determines how efficiently it extracts water and nutrients from soil. Plants with better root systems survive drought better, resist some pathogens more effectively, and ultimately yield more food.
